# An Automatic GPU Low-Precision Vectorization Arena with a Numerical-Stability Gate: A Brutally Honest Assessment

## Executive Summary

The honest verdict: there is one publishable paper here, it is narrower than the draft proposes, and its central claim rests on two experiments that have not been run. Four of the five proposed contributions are incremental re-applications of existing work. The fifth — a per-loop-nest, transform-resolved fast-math safety gate — has a real shot at "substantial novelty," but only if two specific ablations land: (a) that resolving the verdict per transform ({reassociation, FMA-contraction, FTZ}) rather than emitting a single scalar "unstable" verdict recovers materially more speedup than an all-or-nothing gate, and (b) that the safety verdict changes with the chosen vector length because vlen changes the reduction-tree shape. If (b) holds, "floating-point stability is a function of the chosen vector length" is a genuinely unmodeled, quotable result and can anchor a CGO or PPoPP paper on its own. If (b) is null, what remains is Verrou (Correctness@SC 2019) wired into DaCe codegen — a tools/artifact paper, not a research paper.

The draft's own framing is correct that the arena and the vector-length sweep are prior art (MCompiler arXiv 2019; Kernel Tuner accuracy-aware MP, TPDS 2026; NeuroVectorizer, CGO 2020). Where the draft is still too generous to itself: (i) its Claim 1 ("no cost model works") is a cost model in disguise — the proposed learned pruner *is* a cost model, and the coherent "prune then measure" fallback is exactly Ansor's architecture (OSDI 2020), so Claim 1 should be retired as a thesis and demoted to a measured finding; (ii) the safety gate's novelty over Verrou reduces to the two unrun ablations above; (iii) the static solver detector keys on residual-tolerance exit guards, but the most dangerous solvers use fixed iteration counts and have no such guard, so they pass through the detector as "safe" and then diverge under reassociation — the false-negative (silent wrong answer) risk is under-modeled; (iv) the opt-remark feature source is richest on CPU, where it is least needed, and effectively absent on raw CUDA, where it is most needed.

Recommended action: cut to the guarded fast-math gate, run the vlen-coupling experiment before writing anything else, and report the recoverable-speedup frontier under a near-zero false-enable constraint honestly even if that frontier is modest.

## Steelmanned Thesis

The strongest version of the project is a bridge between two communities that do not currently talk: loop-nest and precision autotuning (which searches schedule, vector length, and precision but ignores floating-point semantics) and floating-point numerical analysis (which reasons about rounding and conditioning but does no schedule, vlen, or emission search). The bridge is instantiated in a data-centric IR (DaCe SDFG, SC 2019) that can regenerate every backend from a single representation, which is a structural property none of the single-toolchain competitors possess.

**Claim 1 (arena necessity), narrow form.** For automatic GPU low-precision vectorization, the space of {compiler} × {language} × {GPU backend} × {vector length} × {emission strategy} × {precision} is large, and the fastest correct member per loop nest cannot be picked by any single-compiler static cost model, because each compiler's internal cost model is internal to itself and, like uiCA/Ithemal/GRANITE, takes a fixed instruction stream as input and therefore cannot rank which toolchain will *produce* the better stream. This narrow form is defensible. The absolute form ("no cost model will ever work") is not, and should not be defended — see the answer to hard question (a). In fact this steelman collapses into Claim 2 plus an evaluation, because the coherent remedy the draft proposes (learned pruner, then measure) is itself a cost model. Treat Claim 1 as an evaluation finding, not a thesis.

**Claim 2 (stability-gated predictive mode).** (2a) The winning toolchain for a loop nest can be predicted from cheap features well enough to prune the arena. (2b) A loop nest's floating-point stability can be predicted well enough to apply fast-math, FMA-contraction, and reassociation only where numerically safe, and to choose safe precision per nest. Note that 2b silently conflates three questions the numerical-analysis literature keeps separate: (i) conditioning of the underlying problem (κ(A), a property of the mathematics), (ii) stability of the algorithm (backward error, a property of the code), and (iii) sensitivity to a specific rounding transform (reassociation of *this* reduction). The static idioms detect proxies for (iii); the Higham κ bounds are (i); Verificarlo measures a blend of (ii) and (iii). A well-conditioned problem solved by a catastrophically cancelling algorithm is (i)-safe and (ii)-unsafe, and a κ-based tier will mislabel it. The paper must not treat these as one axis; a numerical-analyst reviewer will hammer the conflation.

## Prior-Art Landscape

**CPU precision autotuning (search-based).** Precimonious (SC 2013) plus Blame Analysis (FSE 2016) established per-variable delta-debugging precision search; HiFPTuner (ISSTA 2018) added dependence-grouped hierarchical search; ADAPT (SC 2018) and FloatSmith/CRAFT/TypeForge (2019) used reverse-mode AD for precision sensitivities; PROMISE/CADNA (CPC 2008/2010) validated via Discrete Stochastic Arithmetic; TAFFO (IEEE Micro 2019 / JSS 2022) did LLVM fixed-point conversion via ILP. All are CPU, single-toolchain, single or double precision (not fp16/bf16/fp8), and not vlen- or emission-aware.

**Rigorous / sound FP tuning and bounding.** FPTuner (POPL 2017, Symbolic Taylor to QCQP with certified bounds), Daisy/Rosa (POPL 2014 / TACAS 2018, interval plus affine), Gappa (TOMS 2010), PRECiSA (SAFECOMP 2017 / v4.0 2024), Satire (SC 2020; mixed-precision loop-free arXiv 2025, ~4M ops/hr). Cost of Soundness (OOPSLA 2025) quantifies the performance lost to soundness. All are essentially loop-free (they unroll) and provably do not scale to iterative solvers.

**GPU mixed-precision tuning.** GPUMixer (ISC 2019, Best Paper — static Fast Imprecise Sets plus shadow error), AMPT-GA (ICS 2019, GA plus cast-aware perf model, beats Precimonious 14–63%), GRAM (TACO 2021, runtime FP16-half2/FP32 mixing), GPU-FPtuner (HiPC 2020, compiler analysis plus error-propagation-pattern awareness). All fp32↔fp64, single-vendor compiler, no vlen sweep, no cross-language emission, no fast-math-transform safety.

**Joint precision × code-optimization empirical tuning.** Kernel Tuner accuracy-aware MP (TPDS 2026) jointly tunes precision, block size, tiling, and vector width on NVIDIA and AMD, and reports joint tuning beats isolated tuning. This is the single biggest threat to Claim 2's "GPU arena that sweeps vlens and precision." PrecTuner (PPoPP 2024) couples polyhedral mixed-precision code generation with tuning but collapses emission to one parameter r, CPU/affine only.

**Learned prediction of MP perf and accuracy.** FPLearner / Wang & Rubio-González (ICSE 2024) — a gated graph neural network over a Precision Interaction Graph predicts both performance and accuracy of an MP config without running, F1 ≈ 96–97%, cutting tuning 25–61%. This is the headline threat to Claim 2b, and evidence that "predict accuracy without running" is already viable.

**Automatic GPU fp16 vectorization.** Ho & Wong (HPEC 2017) — LLVM/LibTooling auto-migrates CUDA fp32 to half/half2, pairs two threads into half2, rewrites strided accesses, masks divergence; 2–3×. It explicitly excludes numerical safety ("outside the scope of this work"); streamcluster fails to converge. vlen fixed at 2, single compiler and language.

**Compiler/vectorization cost models and their failure.** Pohl et al. (MASCOTS 2019 / Parallel Computing 2020): LLVM vectorizer cost model Spearman ρ=0.58, GCC ρ=0.33 on AVX2, both systematically overestimating (~6×). Read carefully: ρ=0.58 is a *moderate positive correlation* — a model that works reasonably, not one that "does not work." goSLP (OOPSLA 2018) and VW-SLP (PACT 2018) document that static SIMD cost models are frequently wrong and that better-than-model schedules exist. Fixed-code throughput predictors: uiCA (ICS 2022, ~1% error, Intel-only), llvm-mca/IACA (19–22%), Ithemal (ICML 2019, 10.5%), GRANITE (IISWC 2022). None take the compiler or language as an input.

**Cross-compiler / cross-language arenas and portability.** MCompiler (arXiv 2019; Springer 2025) — per-loop-nest arena across ICC/pgcc/GCC/Clang/Polly/Pluto with a random-forest predictor on hardware counters (within 4%/8% of exhaustive), 1.96×/2.62× over ICC. ComPar (IWOMP 2020) — empirical per-loop selection across Cetus/AutoPar/Par4All. Pennycook P metric (PMBS@SC 2016 / FGCS 2019). Portability studies (MD-Bench arXiv 2023; "Limits of Performance Portability in Directive-Based GPU Programming" arXiv 2026; P3HPC arXiv 2402.08950) confirm the winning backend/language varies per kernel by up to ~10× and warn that language gaps are often compiler-maturity artifacts (Flang emits all-scalar). This warning is load-bearing against the cross-language axis; see the C2 verdict.

**Autotuning frameworks / learned schedulers.** OpenTuner (PACT 2014), CompilerGym (CGO 2022), MILEPOST GCC (IJPP 2011), AutoTVM (NeurIPS 2018) / Ansor (OSDI 2020) / TenSet (NeurIPS 2021) / TLP (ASPLOS 2023), Halide autoscheduler (SIGGRAPH 2019). Ansor/TLP establish "learned ranker prunes, hardware decides" as the correct architecture and provide the evidence that learned cost models rank well enough to prune but do not eliminate measurement. Cross-hardware transfer failure (TCL 2026; cross-feature transfer MDPI 2024) supports that a static model will not hold across the thesis's cross-compiler/cross-precision axes without retraining.

**Polyhedral / loop-nest optimizers and their FP-blindness.** Pluto (PLDI 2008), PPCG (TACO 2013), Tiramisu (CGO 2019), Polygeist (PACT 2021), isl scheduler, PolyGym (PACT 2021), LoopTune (arXiv 2023). None model FP semantics. The one exception, Polly reduction scheduling (IMPACT 2015), relaxes reduction dependences via associativity/commutativity but requires -ffast-math and performs no numerical analysis — the load-bearing citation for the gap.

**ML-for-vectorization / opt-remark mining.** NeuroVectorizer (CGO 2020, deep-RL over VF∈{1..64}×IF∈{1..16}), Pouchet/Trifunović ML vectorization (TACO 2012), "Predicting Loop Vectorization through ML" (Fusion P&A 2024, RF/FNN/SVM on TSVC via hardware counters). Remark-as-signal: VecTrans (arXiv 2025, LLM reads -Rpass), AutoPass (arXiv 2026, -Rpass-missed evidence), "AI Coding Agents Need Better Compiler Remarks" (arXiv 2026, quantifies remark lift), CompilerGPT (arXiv 2025). All consume remarks as text for LLM or heuristic use; none engineer remark success/failure *rates* as ML features for a cross-compiler winner predictor.

**FP-stability detection.** Dynamic: Verificarlo / Monte-Carlo Arithmetic (ARITH 2016), CADNA/DSA (CPC 2008), Verrou plus delta-debugging (Correctness@SC 2019, isolates stable vs unstable scopes at ~10–50×), NSan (CC 2021, shadow at 2.3–17.2×). On-the-fly test-instability (OOPSLA 2013), Digit-Stability Inference (arXiv 2020). Predictive: EXPLANIFLOAT / condition-numbers plus oracle (ARITH 2025, 80% precision / 96% recall). Root-cause and mitigation: "Beware of fast-math" (Byrne 2020), ReproBLAS/ExBLAS (IPDPS 2013 / IEEE TC 2015), stochastic rounding survey (Roy. Soc. Open Sci. 2022). Solver theory: IR3 (SISC 2018, fp16 LU safe iff κ ≤ ~1e4), GMRES-IR (SISC 2017, relaxes to ~1e8), five-precision GMRES-IR (2021), HPL-MxP (IJHPCA 2026), tensor-core IR (SC18 / Proc. R. Soc. A 2020). Reduction recognition: LRPD (PLDI 1995) — but it frames reductions as reassociation-*safe*, the inverse of the safety concern.

Verrou is the most underrated threat in this list. It already partitions a program into fast-math-stable versus unstable scopes via stochastic-rounding perturbation and bisection, which is precisely "classify a scope as fast-math-safe." Because perturbing rounding *is* the reassociation/FTZ danger model, running Verrou with FMA and reassociation toggled gives a transform-resolved answer today with a shell loop. Any novelty claim against Verrou must therefore rest on vlen-awareness and codegen integration, not on "transform-resolved" alone.

**Substrate.** DaCe SDFG (SC 2019; Data-Centric Python arXiv 2021) — one IR to CPU/GPU/FPGA. Not itself an arena or a safety gate; the contribution layers on top.

## SoTA Blind Spots

**(i) Loop-nest optimizers assume FP associativity.** Pluto, PPCG, Tiramisu, Polygeist, isl, LoopTune, PolyGym, Halide/Ansor treat schedule legality as value-preserving. Their legality abstraction (integer sets, dependence graphs) has no representation of rounding error or condition number. Polly reduction scheduling (IMPACT 2015) is the closest and gates on the -ffast-math flag with zero numerical analysis — it reassociated 52 Polybench reductions treating -ffast-math as free. The intersection {reorders FP reductions} ∩ {analyzes whether that reorder is numerically safe} is empty in this literature. This is the real gap and it is narrow.

**(ii) Autotuners ignore numerical stability, and none resolve the verdict by transform or by vlen.** Reassociation, FMA-contraction, and FTZ have different danger profiles: reassociation destroys compensated summation and changes reduction-tree error; FMA-contraction usually *helps* accuracy; FTZ hurts only near underflow. Prior tools output a scalar "unstable" verdict at best (Verrou, Verificarlo). Critically, stability is plausibly a function of vlen because vlen changes reduction-tree shape — a coupling nobody models. This is the exploitable gap, and it is also unverified. It must be demonstrated, not asserted.

**(iii) Cost models versus cross-compiler reality.** Every static or learned cost model surveyed is single-compiler and/or fixed-code, so none can predict which of gcc/clang/nvc++/icx/nvfortran × CUDA/OpenACC/OpenMP-target will produce the better stream. Empirical support that the winner varies per kernel: MD-Bench (ICC 2.83s vs Clang 4.32 vs ICX 5.04 vs GCC 5.75), LCALS, directive-portability arXiv 2026 (~10× kernel-level gaps). Caveat that weakens (iii) as a headline: the per-nest winner is unstable across input size and shape (MD-Bench shows the winner flipping with working-set-to-cache regime), so this is an argument for measuring, not an argument that a predictor is impossible.

## Ranked Contributions with Novelty Verdicts

### C1 — Per-loop-nest, transform-resolved, codegen-integrated FP-safety gate
**Claim.** A hybrid static plus cheap-dynamic classifier that, per loop nest, decides which of {reassociation, FMA-contraction, FTZ, low-precision accumulation} is admissible, and gates DaCe emission accordingly — vetoing fast-math on iterative-solver / residual-tolerance / compensated-reduction idioms and enabling it on streaming/reducible nests.

**Novelty verdict: incremental-to-substantial, and substantial only conditional on two unrun ablations.** Prior art: Polly-reductions (IMPACT 2015) reorders reductions with zero numerical analysis and relies on the user's -ffast-math flag. FPTuner/Daisy/Satire give sound bounds but are loop-free and cannot handle iterative solvers. Verrou/Verificarlo/CADNA detect instability dynamically, per-input, at 3–50× cost, and produce a scalar verdict. FPLearner (ICSE 2024) predicts an MP config's accuracy but does not classify structural fast-math danger. Herbie local error (PLDI 2015) and EXPLANIFLOAT (ARITH 2025) are already predictive instability classifiers. LRPD (PLDI 1995) detects reductions to mark them safe-to-parallelize — the inverse framing. The residual over Verrou is *transform-resolution* and *vlen-awareness* wired into codegen. Transform-resolution is thin as a scientific delta, because Verrou can be scripted to toggle FMA/reassociation. The claim survives only if: (a) transform-resolution recovers materially more speedup than a scalar verdict (if FMA-recovered speedup is ~3%, C1 collapses to "Verrou wired into codegen"), and (b) the safety verdict actually changes with vlen. Both are ablations #2 and #3 below; both are currently assertions.

**Risk.** The paper is bet on two experiments not yet run. If ablation #3 shows verdicts are vlen-invariant in practice, the "SIMD-aware stability" pillar — the only thing that distinguishes C1 from Verrou — evaporates and there is no CGO paper. Run ablation #3 first.

### C2 — Joint {compiler × language × backend × vlen × precision × fast-math} arena over one IR
**Claim.** A per-loop-nest arena that regenerates the same algorithm from one SDFG into C/C++/Fortran and CUDA/OpenACC/OpenMP-target, compiled by gcc/clang/nvc++/icx/nvfortran, sweeping vlen and fp16/bf16/fp8, selecting empirically per nest.

**Novelty verdict: incremental.** MCompiler is CPU/C-only, full precision, no GPU, no cross-language, source-extraction not one-IR. Kernel Tuner (TPDS 2026) sweeps precision × vlen × block on GPU across NVIDIA and AMD but single-vendor compiler and no cross-language. PrecTuner collapses emission to one parameter, CPU/affine. ComPar (IWOMP 2020) does empirical per-loop multi-tool selection. The only unclaimed coordinate is "cross-compiler AND cross-language from one IR."

**Risk.** The fatal reviewer question: does cross-compiler/cross-language variance produce a different winner often enough to matter, after vlen/precision/backend are already swept? If nvc++ wins most GPU nests regardless of language, C2 is a null result dressed as breadth. Worse, the P3HPC citation (arXiv 2402.08950) warns cross-language gaps are compiler-maturity artifacts — the cross-language axis risks measuring Flang's immaturity rather than a real degree of freedom, i.e. publishing Flang bug reports. The one-IR framing does not rescue this, because backend maturity still dominates. Mitigation: run a pilot showing that language flips the winner independent of Flang immaturity before committing to the axis. Position C2 as the substrate and evaluation vehicle for C1, not as a standalone contribution.

### C3 — Learned pruner over opt-remark features
**Claim.** Engineer LLVM opt-record YAML / GCC -fopt-info / NV -Minfo / ptxas -v into structured features (per-loop vectorized?, chosen vlen, miss-reason class, SLP-NotBeneficial counts, register/occupancy) and train a ranker that predicts the arena winner well enough to compile and run only the top-few candidates, then measure.

**Novelty verdict: incremental, and load-bearing on a brittle feature source.** FPLearner/NeuroVectorizer/MCompiler/MILEPOST use IR features or hardware counters, not remark success/failure rates, and predict within one toolchain. VecTrans/AutoPass/CompilerGPT consume remarks as text for an LLM. The residual — remark rates as structured cross-compiler features — is real but weak-signal.

**Risk (higher than the draft admits).** LLVM opt-record schema and miss-reason strings change between releases, so the feature extractor is a maintenance treadmill and the dataset rots. GCC -fopt-info, LLVM -Rpass, and nvc++ -Minfo have incommensurable taxonomies; any shared "miss-reason class" is a hand-built lossy mapping that injects bias into the very features meant to predict the winner. Raw nvcc emits essentially no source-level vectorization remarks, so on the most important backend the headline feature source is absent and the fallback (ptxas register/occupancy) is a latency proxy that says nothing about FP safety. The remark-feature story is strongest on CPU, where it is least needed, and absent on CUDA, where it is most needed. Keep C3 as at most one feature-ablation; do not build the predictive story on it.

### C4 — Auto-deploy-the-right-mitigation policy for danger-flagged reductions
**Claim.** For nests C1 flags dangerous, add arena candidates using stochastic rounding, compensated/ReproBLAS-style reproducible reduction, or a low-precision-compute plus high-precision-IR wrapper — an automatic policy that today exists only as human judgment.

**Novelty verdict: incremental (engineering), and infeasible beyond a demonstrator as stated.** SR (Roy. Soc. 2022), ReproBLAS (IPDPS 2013), IR3/GMRES-IR (SISC 2017/18) are hand-invoked mitigations; HPL-MxP is one curated kernel. Wiring "detect dangerous reduction → emit mitigation" into a selector is a plausible systems contribution.

**Risk.** Auto-emitting a *correct* GMRES-IR wrapper for an arbitrary detected "solver" requires knowing it is a linear solve, extracting A, and choosing five precisions — that is program synthesis, not idiom rewriting, and is infeasible for anything but recognized dense LU. C4 works for the demo kernels and nothing else. Restrict C4 to a single demonstrator (dense LU + IR) and say so explicitly.

### C5 — Public cross-compiler/cross-precision measurement dataset
**Claim.** Release the analog of BHive/TenSet spanning compilers × languages × backends × precisions × emission strategies, with per-nest max-error-vs-oracle.

**Novelty verdict: none as science, genuine as artifact.** Fine as a supporting bullet; cannot anchor a paper. Risk: its generation cost is the exhaustive training arena and is quietly enormous (see Threats to Validity).

**Ranking rationale.** Only C1 can reach "substantial," conditional on ablations #2 and #3. C2 is the substrate. C3, C4, C5 are supporting. Lead with C1; position C2 as the evaluation vehicle that produces the vlen-coupling evidence; demote everything else.

## Answers to the Four Hard Questions

### (a) Is "no cost model works" defensible?
Only the narrow form, and even that must be reframed. The proposed remedy — a learned pruner that ranks toolchains from features (C3) — *is* a cost model, so "no cost model works" is self-refuting as stated. The coherent position is "no static single-compiler cost model can rank a cross-compiler winner (uiCA, Ithemal, GRANITE all predict throughput of a *given* stream), so measurement is needed for the final decision, but a learned model can rank to prune." That position is exactly Ansor's architecture applied to a new axis set, which means Claim 1 contributes nothing beyond "the axis set is new" — and that is C2. Retire Claim 1 as a claim; report it as a measured finding: the cross-compiler/cross-language/precision winner distribution, and how much a single-toolchain choice leaves on the table. Do not cite Pohl's ρ=0.58 as evidence that "no cost model works" — 0.58 is a moderate positive correlation, and it would be embarrassing to cite it as proof of failure while proposing a predictor you would be pleased to bring to 0.7.

### (b) Predictive-mode design: which toolchain won.
Feature sources, cheapest first: (1) opt-remark features — parse LLVM opt-record YAML, GCC -fopt-info, NV -Minfo, ptxas -v; extract per-loop vectorized yes/no, chosen VF/IF, miss-reason histogram, SLP NotBeneficial counts, GPU register pressure/occupancy. These are dual-use: a dependence/reduction bailout correlates with both speed and danger and feeds C1. (2) IR features — reuse IR2Vec (TACO 2020) or ProGraML (ICML 2021) as the embedding backbone. (3) static structural features — trip-count class, stride pattern, arithmetic-to-cast ratio (GPUMixer's FISet insight). Target a per-instance ranker (SATzilla-style, JAIR 2008) predicting the top-k toolchains. Realistic accuracy: coarse 2-way choices reach ~88% (DeepTune) to ~98% (IR2Vec); FPLearner reaches F1 ≈ 96–97% on binary labels. N-way cross-compiler winner prediction and continuous runtime regression are markedly harder and weakly validated — claim pruning to a small candidate set at low regret, not picking the winner.

Two risks the design must confront before it is credible. First, the label is ill-defined: the per-nest winner is a function of input size, array shape, and conditioning (MD-Bench shows the winner flipping with cache regime), so the predictor fits a moving target. Report label stability — how often the winner is identical across at least three input sizes — before claiming 2a is predictable; if stability is below ~70%, 2a is dead and must be dropped as a headline. Second, the feature source is compiler-version-brittle and vendor-incommensurable, and absent on raw CUDA (see C3 risk).

### (c) Per-loop-nest FP-stability estimation: method and cost.
A tiered pipeline, cheapest first:
1. **Static idiom analysis (near-free).** Detect compensated-summation fingerprints (`t=s+y; e=(t-s)-y`), pairwise/tree reductions, loop-carried FP recurrences, subtraction of near-equal magnitudes, and division by dependence-carried values. This is what "Beware of fast-math" (Byrne 2020) and ReproBLAS root-cause (non-associativity) say to look for. Reliable for compensated-sum (syntactic, low false-positive) and reduction detection; unreliable for cancellation and ill-conditioning, which are runtime properties (see hard question d).
2. **Condition-number / affine-error single-iteration estimate (cheap).** Per-op condition numbers (Herbie idea; EXPLANIFLOAT, ARITH 2025, 80% precision / 96% recall) or a one-iteration affine estimate (Daisy). For linear-solve nests use κ(A) directly: fp16 safe iff κ ≤ ~1e4 (IR3, SISC 2018), relaxed to ~1e8 under GMRES-IR (SISC 2017). Risk: κ measures problem conditioning, not algorithm stability — a well-conditioned problem with a cancelling algorithm will be mislabeled safe. State the (i)/(ii)/(iii) distinction explicitly.
3. **Cheap dynamic confirmation on borderline nests (NSan shadow).** Risk the draft understates: NSan's cost is 2.3–17.2×, and the 17.2× figure is for the double→fp128 shadow — exactly the fp64 codes that dominate scientific kernels — so the "cheap backstop" is 17× for the common case, not 2.3×. NSan also needs representative inputs; for a solver, instability may only appear near convergence at iteration 400, which a sample input may never reach.
4. **Offline ground-truth labeling (train-time only).** Verificarlo-MCA (ARITH 2016) and CADNA (CPC 2008). Risk: labeling cost is tens of runs × per-op overhead × hundreds of nests × the {precision × fast-math × vlen} cross-product — a large and unbudgeted compute bill.

Be explicit that purely static soundness for iterative solvers is infeasible (Satire/FPTuner prove sound bounds do not scale past unrolled loops), so the gate is a conservative predictor with a dynamic backstop, not a proof.

### (d) Detecting data-dependent loops and solvers too dangerous for fast-math.
This is where the draft is most over-optimistic. Decidability splits sharply, and the false-negative cost inverts the risk economics.

Reliable to detect: the **compensated-summation idiom** (Kahan/Neumaier error-term subtraction) is a syntactic fingerprint that reassociation provably destroys — high-confidence, low false-positive. This is the best and most honest example, with the caveat that it is also the case the compiler already mishandles and that much real code does not use Kahan at all. **Explicit reductions** `acc = acc ⊕ f(...)` are reliably detectable (LRPD-style), and classifying them unsafe-to-reassociate in low precision is a defensible heuristic.

Unreliable / practically undecidable: the draft's iterative-solver signature ("loop-carried scalar computed by a reduction feeds the exit guard") has high false-positives (any convergence-monitoring loop, including fast-math-safe ones whose residual sits well above roundoff) and — the buried problem — high false-negatives. Solvers with a **fixed iteration count** (`for k in range(50)`: Newton with a fixed budget, Chebyshev iteration, fixed-sweep multigrid) have no residual guard and sail through the detector as "safe," then diverge under reassociation. The most dangerous solvers are the ones without a data-dependent trip count, and the detector keys on the wrong feature. Data-dependent control flow (open question A): detecting that a branch derives from an FP value is easy; detecting that the branch is *unstable* is the PRECiSA/ReFlow problem (LOPSTR 2018 / iFM 2020), a static over-approximation that does not scale past small kernels and floods the gate with conservative false vetoes on every pivot, argmax, and threshold. Cancellation and ill-conditioning are runtime properties; statically one can only flag "a subtraction exists," which is uselessly broad.

The asymmetry the design must center: a **false-enable** (fast-math on a truly-unsafe nest) is a silent wrong answer in a scientific code — the worst failure, unbounded downstream cost. A **false-veto** costs some speedup. The gate must therefore be tuned to ~zero false-enable, which forces heavy over-vetoing, which means little fast-math speedup is recovered on anything that looks solver-like — and solvers are where the low-precision speedups actually live (that is why HPL-MxP exists). Quantify the recoverable-speedup-under-zero-false-enable frontier early; if it is small, the gate is academically interesting and practically inert. Report it honestly regardless.

## Experimental Design

**Benchmarks.**
- Polybench/C and NPBench (affine dense nests; largely reassociation-safe, where fast-math should be enabled). Bit-exact CPU+GPU harness already exists in the DaCe worktree.
- TSVC / TSVC_2 (155+ idioms; standard for vectorization-benefit studies, used by NeuroVectorizer, VecTrans, Fusion 2024 — enables direct baseline comparison).
- A dedicated solver "danger set": CG/BiCGSTAB/GMRES, Newton iterations, Jacobi/Gauss-Seidel/SOR, compensated (Kahan/pairwise) summation, plus HPL-MxP-style LU+IR. This is where unguarded fast-math must be shown to break. Corpus-bias risk (see Threats): the danger set is hand-picked and chosen knowing the answer, so the detector is being tested on the idioms it was written for. A held-out, third-party solver corpus (SuiteSparse-driven, or real mini-app inner loops) with danger labels not pre-known is required to break the circularity. Include fixed-iteration-count solvers deliberately, since those are the detector's blind spot.
- Optionally LULESH and LBM mini-apps for external validity.

**Backends/toolchains.** Emit each nest as C/C++/Fortran × CUDA/OpenACC/OpenMP-target from one SDFG; compile with gcc, clang, nvc++, icx, nvfortran; sweep vlen ∈ {scalar, 2, 4, 8}, precision ∈ {fp32, fp16, bf16, fp8} where legal, fast-math level ∈ {none, FMA-only, reassoc, full}. Pin GPU arch=89; run compiled kernels forked/isolated to survive CUDA segfaults. fp8 caveat: fp8 dynamic range is tiny, so most general-loop-nest fp8 configs will overflow/NaN rather than run; restrict fp8 claims to tensor-core-GEMM-shaped kernels and state so.

**Search-space caveat.** 5 compilers × 3 languages × 3 backends × 4 vlens × 4 precisions × 4 fast-math levels ≈ 2,880 configs per nest; even at 5% legal that is ~140 compile-and-run per nest × hundreds of nests, with nvc++/icx compilation dominating. The pruner must cut >95%, but it can only be trained after the full-arena cost is paid on the training corpus. The "arena cost reduction at fixed regret" metric must not hide that the training arena was exhaustive.

**Baselines.**
- Claim 1 finding: MCompiler (CPU/C arena), Kernel Tuner accuracy-aware MP, OpenTuner/CompilerGym, Ansor — each single-toolchain, to show the cross-compiler/cross-language axis is unspanned. Fairness risk: these run on their own corpora and tuning budgets; a fair head-to-head requires porting, or reviewers reject the comparison as apples-to-oranges.
- Claim 2a: NeuroVectorizer, Pohl portable cost model, MILEPOST.
- Claim 2b: GPUMixer, AMPT-GA, GPU-FPtuner, FPLearner, plus unguarded -ffast-math (fast, wrong on solvers) and always-safe (correct, slow), to show the gate dominates the accuracy-vs-speed frontier on solvers.

**Metrics.**
- Per-FP-mode max-diff-vs-oracle — the headline correctness metric. Oracle caveat (see Threats): fp64 is not ground truth for an ill-conditioned solver; use MPFR/double-double, and define "correct" for a solver as "converges to the right answer," not "bit-close per iteration."
- Speedup and energy per nest and end-to-end vs fp64-only and vs single-best-compiler.
- Pennycook P across the platform set.
- Arena cost reduction at fixed regret (C3 metric), reported without hiding training-arena cost.
- Gate confusion matrix vs Verificarlo/NSan labels: false-enable rate (must be ~0) and false-veto rate (recoverable-speedup loss).

**Ablations (run #3 first — it is the load-bearing wall).**
1. Gate on/off: count solver kernels that break under unguarded fast-math.
2. Transform resolution: scalar "unstable" verdict vs {reassoc/FMA/FTZ} — quantify speedup recovered by not vetoing FMA. If this is ~3%, C1 collapses.
3. **vlen-coupling: fix stability verdict vs recompute per vlen.** If the verdict changes with vlen, "stability is a function of vector length" is the paper's central novel result. If it does not, the SIMD-aware pillar is gone.
4. Pruner feature ablation: opt-remark vs IR2Vec-only vs hardware-counters-only, with leave-one-benchmark-out to prevent family-level winner memorization.
5. Static-only gate vs static+dynamic-backstop.
6. Cross-compiler/cross-language variance study, including label stability across ≥3 input sizes (the precondition for 2a).

**Target venue.** CGO or PPoPP, but only if ablations #2 and #3 land. If #3 is null, the result is "Verrou wired into DaCe" — a tools/artifact paper. ICS is a viable second choice; SC fits only if the HPL-MxP-style mixed-precision-at-scale story (C4) becomes the headline, which the C4 feasibility risk argues against.

## Threats to Validity

- **Corpus bias / circularity.** Polybench/NPBench/TSVC are affine, well-conditioned, non-iterative — the fast-math-safe regime — so the gate looks great because there is nothing dangerous to catch. The danger set is hand-picked with known labels, so the idiom detector is evaluated on the idioms it was written for. Requires a held-out third-party corpus with unknown danger labels.
- **Oracle definition.** fp64 is not ground truth for chaotic or ill-conditioned iterative solvers; MPFR/double-double is required. A reassociated solver that converges in 51 iterations instead of 50 to the same answer is fine but shows large per-iteration diff, so a per-iteration max-diff metric mislabels benign reorderings as unsafe. The metric is wrong for the danger set unless "correct" is defined as convergence to the right answer.
- **Baseline fairness.** MCompiler/Kernel Tuner run on their own corpora and budgets; skipping a port makes the comparison apples-to-oranges.
- **Hardware monoculture.** A single RTX 4050 / arch 89 is a consumer Ada laptop GPU. Cross-compiler GPU and low-precision claims generalize poorly from one device; tensor-core fp8 differs on Hopper/Blackwell. Reviewers will discount GPU low-precision claims validated on one GPU.
- **Pruner train/test leakage.** Same-benchmark-family nests in train and test let the pruner memorize family-level winners; use leave-one-benchmark-out.
- **fp8 legality.** Most general-loop-nest fp8 configs measure overflow/NaN, not speedup. The honest fp8 story is tensor-core GEMM only.
- **Label instability.** The per-nest winner moves with input size, shape, and hardware; the predictor fits a moving target unless label stability is measured and high.
- **Labeling compute budget.** MCA/CADNA labeling scales with the config cross-product and is a large, currently unbudgeted cost.
- **Feature rot.** Opt-remark schemas change across compiler releases and are vendor-incommensurable; the dataset and extractor decay over time.

## The One Strongest Paper to Write, and the Framings to Drop

**Framings to drop.**
- Claim 1 ("no cost model works") as a thesis. It is Ansor's architecture on a new axis set and a cost model in disguise. Demote to an evaluation finding.
- C3 (opt-remark features) as a contribution. Brittle, vendor-incommensurable, absent on CUDA. Keep as at most one ablation.
- C4 (auto-mitigation / auto-GMRES-IR) beyond a single dense-LU demonstrator. Automatic correct solver-wrapper synthesis is a separate research program.
- The cross-language axis, unless a pilot first shows that language (compiler fixed, one IR) flips the winner independent of Flang immaturity. Otherwise it is a Flang bug report.
- fp8 for general loop nests. Restrict to tensor-core-shaped kernels.
- "Predict which compiler won" (2a) as a headline, unless label stability across sizes is measured and high.

**The paper.**

> Numerically-guarded fast-math: a per-loop-nest gate that recognizes reassociation-unsafe reduction idioms and admits fast-math/FMA/low-precision only where a cheap static-plus-MCA screen certifies it, integrated into a data-centric codegen backend.

Scoped down and made rigorous:
1. Lead with the transform-resolved, reassociation-focused gate (C1), but validate the two distinguishing pillars first and prominently: (a) transform-resolution buys real speedup (FMA-enabled-while-reassoc-vetoed recovers X%), and (b) vlen changes the safety verdict via reduction-tree shape. If (b) holds, that single result — stability is a function of the chosen vector length — is a publishable, quotable contribution on its own, and nobody models it.
2. Ground truth honestly: static idioms (compensated-sum fingerprint, reliable; reduction classification, heuristic) plus an NSan/MCA backstop, with Verificarlo/CADNA as labelers. Report the false-enable rate driven to ~0 and the resulting recoverable-speedup frontier — an honest, possibly modest frontier is a strong paper; an overclaimed universal speedup is a desk-reject. Be explicit that fixed-iteration-count solvers are a known blind spot of the residual-guard detector.
3. Position the arena (C2) as the substrate and evaluation vehicle, not the contribution: DaCe emits each variant from one IR, so the safety/perf frontier the gate navigates can be measured, and the vlen-coupling evidence in (1b) can be produced.
4. Explicitly subsume-and-cite Polly-reductions (does the reorder, not the safety), Verrou (does the scope classification, not transform-resolved/vlen-aware/codegen-wired), FPLearner (predicts config accuracy, not structural transform-danger), and Higham IR3/GMRES-IR (the theory operationalized for the LU case only).

Run the vlen-coupling experiment before writing anything else. It is the load-bearing wall; everything the draft calls "strongest" rests on it, and it is currently an unverified assertion.

## Annotated Bibliography

- **Polly reduction scheduling (IMPACT 2015).** Reorders 52 Polybench FP reductions via associativity, gated on the -ffast-math flag with zero numerical analysis. The load-bearing citation for the gap C1 fills, and the exact prior art C1 must beat.
- **Verrou + delta-debugging (Correctness@SC 2019).** Partitions a program into fast-math-stable vs unstable scopes via stochastic-rounding perturbation and bisection at ~10–50×. The most underrated threat to C1; novelty over it must rest on vlen-awareness and codegen integration, not "transform-resolved."
- **Verificarlo / Monte-Carlo Arithmetic (ARITH 2016).** MCA-based instability detection. Use as offline labeler; too slow for an online gate.
- **NSan (CC 2021).** Shadow-execution numerical sanitizer at 2.3–17.2×. The 17.2× is the double→fp128 common case, so the "cheap backstop" is not cheap for fp64 kernels; needs representative inputs.
- **CADNA / DSA (CPC 2008/2010).** Discrete Stochastic Arithmetic. Offline labeler alongside Verificarlo.
- **PRECiSA / ReFlow (SAFECOMP 2017; LOPSTR 2018 / iFM 2020).** Static over-approximation of unstable guards. Reused for open-question-A veto features, not invented here; does not scale past small kernels and over-vetoes.
- **EXPLANIFLOAT (ARITH 2025).** Condition-number-plus-oracle instability classifier, 80% precision / 96% recall. Prior predictive classifier; C1 must differentiate on transform/vlen resolution.
- **FPLearner (ICSE 2024).** GGNN over a Precision Interaction Graph predicts MP config perf and accuracy without running, F1 ≈ 96–97%. Headline threat to Claim 2b; shows "predict without running" is viable.
- **FPTuner (POPL 2017); Daisy/Rosa (POPL 2014 / TACAS 2018); Satire (SC 2020).** Sound FP error bounding, loop-free (they unroll). Prove sound static bounds do not scale to iterative solvers — the reason C1 must be a predictor with a dynamic backstop, not a proof.
- **Herbie (PLDI 2015).** Local-error-driven expression rewriting; the per-op condition-number idea used in stability tier 2.
- **LRPD (PLDI 1995).** Runtime reduction/privatization detection, framed as reassociation-*safe*. C1 inverts the framing to unsafe-to-reassociate.
- **MCompiler (arXiv 2019 / Springer 2025).** Per-loop-nest cross-compiler arena (ICC/GCC/Clang/Polly/Pluto) with RF predictor on hardware counters, within 4–8% of exhaustive, 1.96–2.62× over ICC. CPU/C only. Primary prior art for C2/C3.
- **Kernel Tuner accuracy-aware MP (TPDS 2026).** Joint precision × vlen × block on NVIDIA and AMD; joint beats isolated. The single biggest threat to C2's GPU precision+vlen sweep.
- **PrecTuner (PPoPP 2024).** Polyhedral MP codegen plus tuning, emission collapsed to one parameter, CPU/affine. Prior art for the codegen+tuning framing.
- **ComPar (IWOMP 2020).** Empirical per-loop selection across Cetus/AutoPar/Par4All. Cross-tool arena prior art.
- **GPUMixer (ISC 2019); AMPT-GA (ICS 2019); GRAM (TACO 2021); GPU-FPtuner (HiPC 2020).** GPU fp32↔fp64 MP tuners, single-vendor, no vlen sweep, no cross-language, no fast-math-transform safety. Baselines for Claim 2b. GPUMixer's Fast Imprecise Sets and cast-ratio feature feed the pruner.
- **Ho & Wong (HPEC 2017).** LLVM auto-migration of CUDA fp32 to half/half2, 2–3×, explicitly excluding numerical safety; streamcluster fails to converge. The concrete example of what happens without a safety gate.
- **Pohl et al. (MASCOTS 2019 / Parallel Computing 2020).** LLVM vectorizer cost model ρ=0.58, GCC ρ=0.33, ~6× overestimation. 0.58 is a moderate correlation — cite as "weak within one compiler," not "no cost model works."
- **uiCA (ICS 2022, ~1%); Ithemal (ICML 2019, 10.5%); GRANITE (IISWC 2022); llvm-mca/IACA (19–22%).** Fixed-code throughput predictors; none take compiler/language as input. Evidence for the narrow form of Claim 1.
- **goSLP (OOPSLA 2018); VW-SLP (PACT 2018).** Static SIMD cost models frequently wrong; better-than-model schedules exist.
- **Ansor (OSDI 2020); AutoTVM (NeurIPS 2018); TLP (ASPLOS 2023).** "Learned ranker prunes, hardware decides." The architecture C3 actually implements, and the reason Claim 1 is not novel as a thesis.
- **NeuroVectorizer (CGO 2020).** Deep-RL over VF/IF. Baseline for Claim 2a; uses IR features, not remark rates.
- **IR2Vec (TACO 2020); ProGraML (ICML 2021).** IR embeddings; the pruner's embedding backbone.
- **VecTrans (arXiv 2025); AutoPass (arXiv 2026); CompilerGPT (arXiv 2025).** Consume -Rpass/-fopt-info as LLM text, not structured cross-compiler features. Prior art bounding C3's residual.
- **P3HPC directive-portability (arXiv 2402.08950); MD-Bench (arXiv 2023); "Limits of Performance Portability" (arXiv 2026).** Per-kernel winner varies up to ~10×; cross-language gaps are often compiler-maturity artifacts (Flang all-scalar); winner flips with cache regime. Supports C2's premise and simultaneously threatens the cross-language axis and label stability.
- **Pennycook P metric (PMBS@SC 2016 / FGCS 2019).** Portability metric for reporting selected configs across platforms.
- **IR3 (SISC 2018); GMRES-IR (SISC 2017); five-precision GMRES-IR (2021); HPL-MxP (IJHPCA 2026); tensor-core IR (SC18 / Proc. R. Soc. A 2020).** fp16 LU safe iff κ ≤ ~1e4; GMRES-IR relaxes to ~1e8. The theory C1 operationalizes for the LU case and the mitigation C4 would auto-deploy — feasible only for recognized dense LU.
- **ReproBLAS/ExBLAS (IPDPS 2013 / IEEE TC 2015); stochastic rounding survey (Roy. Soc. Open Sci. 2022); "Beware of fast-math" (Byrne 2020).** Non-associativity root-cause and hand-invoked mitigations; the menu C4 would automate and the checklist stability tier 1 pattern-matches.
- **SATzilla (JAIR 2008).** Per-instance algorithm selection via empirical performance models. The template for the pruner, and the reason the arena-selection framing reads as domain re-application.
- **DaCe SDFG (SC 2019); Data-Centric Python (arXiv 2021).** One IR to CPU/GPU/FPGA. The substrate that makes C2 possible; not itself an arena or a gate.

## Addendum: Offloading Granularity and CPU-as-Accelerator

### Prior art by family

**Uniform-device programming models.** These fix a kernel and expose the CPU as one selectable device among GPUs, but neither auto-select the offload unit nor sweep numeric configuration.
- OpenCL platform/device model — Khronos 1.0, 2008; Stone, Gohara, Shi, IEEE CiSE 2010. CPU enumerated as `CL_DEVICE_TYPE_CPU` on the same `clEnqueueNDRangeKernel` path as a GPU.
- SYCL / oneAPI DPC++ — Khronos SYCL 1.2, 2015; DPC++ ~2020. CPU is `info::device_type::cpu`, selectable identically to gpu/accelerator/fpga (https://github.com/oneapi-src/DPCPP_Reference/blob/dpcpp/source/iface/device.rst).
- Kokkos — Edwards, Trott, Sunderland, JPDC 74(12), 2014; machine model at https://kokkos.org/kokkos-core-wiki/ProgrammingGuide/Machine-Model.html. CPU is the Serial/OpenMP ExecutionSpace, a compile-time peer of Cuda/HIP.
- RAJA — Beckingsale et al., P3HPC @ SC 2019. CPU vs GPU is a static execution-policy typedef.
- Alpaka — Zenker et al., IEEE IPDPSW 2016, arXiv:1602.08477. CPU backends are literally named `AccCpuSerial`/`AccCpuOmp2Blocks` (the "Acc" = accelerator).
- OCCA/OKL — Medina, St-Cyr, Warburton, arXiv:1403.0968, 2014. Backend ("Serial"/"OpenMP"/"CUDA") chosen at runtime by JIT — the closest mainstream analog to a runtime backend sweep.
- OpenACC multicore — PGI/NVIDIA ~2015, https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/performance-portability-gpus-cpus-openacc/. The same directive-annotated nest compiles to GPU or CPU via `-ta=multicore`.
- OpenMP target offload — OpenMP 4.0 (2013) / 5.0 (2018) `OMP_TARGET_OFFLOAD`, https://www.openmp.org/spec-html/5.0/openmpse65.html. Host is the default device and automatic fallback, i.e. a device but a fallback rather than a tuned peer.

**Automatic offload-granularity selection.** These choose the loop-nest-scale unit by static or search-based means, but keep precision fixed and (mostly) target the GPU only.
- PPCG — Verdoolaege et al., ACM TACO 2013 / PACT 2013, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2400682.2400713. Kernel = outermost tilable band of the polyhedral schedule tree; one nest can emit multiple kernels; GPU-only, host is orchestrator.
- C-to-CUDA (Pluto-style) — Baskaran, Ramanujam, Sadayappan, CC 2010. Kernel = outermost parallel loop after affine transformation.
- Mint — Unat, Cai, Baden, ICS 2011. Loop nest as unit; parallelization depth/granularity are user-annotated.
- Par4All — Amini et al., IMPACT 2012. Array-region analysis outlines parallel nests; the same unit emits to OpenMP (CPU) or CUDA/OpenCL (GPU) by a global backend flag.
- OpenMPC / OpenMP-to-GPGPU — Lee & Eigenmann, PPoPP 2009 / SC 2010. Each OpenMP parallel region is outlined into one CUDA kernel; a tuning stage searches compilation variants.
- DawnCC — Mendonca et al., ACM TACO 2017. Inserts OpenACC/OpenMP-target pragmas on loops whose access bounds range analysis can prove; has no profitability model and can pick loops that run slower on GPU than CPU.
- CGCM + DyManD — Jablin et al., PLDI 2011 / CGO 2012. Manages the data-movement envelope around an already-chosen offload unit; does not pick the unit.
- GROPHECY — Meng et al., SC 2011, https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2063384.2063402. Projects performance of a skeletonized loop region and picks the fastest among CPU (Xeon), Kepler GPU, and Xeon Phi — host and accelerators as candidate targets in one comparison.
- Polly-ACC — Grosser & Hoefler, ICS 2016. Auto-detects offloadable SCoPs (static-control loop nests) with a polyhedral profitability + data-footprint heuristic and keeps unprofitable nests on the CPU. Strongest single prior art for auto loop-nest granularity plus CPU keep-back.

**Heterogeneous task/loop runtimes and autotuners.** These schedule the same unit to CPU or GPU under a cost model, with the CPU as a peer worker.
- StarPU — Augonnet, Thibault, Namyst, Wacrenier, Euro-Par 2009 / CCPE 2011, https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-642-03869-3_80.pdf. A codelet bundles per-device implementations; an auto-calibrated per-codelet perfmodel (dmda/HEFT) selects the worker. CPU core is one implementation-table entry.
- OmpSs / Nanos++ + OpenMP `declare variant` — Duran et al., PPL 2011; OpenMP 5.0, 2018. One logical task carries CPU and GPU variants; the runtime selects and, with co-execution, splits one task across CPU and GPU.
- GCC function multiversioning / `target_clones` — GCC 6, 2016, https://gcc.gnu.org/onlinedocs/gcc/Function-Multiversioning.html. Per-target clones dispatched by an ifunc feature check — variant emission and selection, but by ISA predicate, not measurement.
- Legion / Realm — Bauer et al., SC 2012; Treichler et al., PACT 2014, https://legion.stanford.edu/tutorial/realm/machine_model.html. CPU is `LOC_PROC`, one Processor kind among `TOC_PROC`(GPU)/`OMP_PROC`; task variants per kind selected by a Mapper.
- PaRSEC / DPLASMA — Bosilca et al., IPDPS 2012, IJHPCA 2013/2025. PTG micro-tasks scheduled onto CPU cores and GPUs as peers by locality/data-reuse heuristics.
- Qilin — Luk, Hong, Kim, MICRO 2009, https://groups.csail.mit.edu/EVO-DesignOpt/pb/uploads/Site/QilinMicro09.pdf. A trained linear per-PE model computes the optimal CPU:GPU split of one data-parallel op.
- PetaBricks / PetaBricks-GPU — Ansel et al., PLDI 2009; Phothilimthana et al., ASPLOS 2013. First-class algorithmic choice with an empirical autotuner that maps and splits work across CPU and GPU; tunes accuracy-vs-time for variable-accuracy algorithms (not bit-level low-precision lowering).
- SkePU 2 / hybrid — Ernstsson, Kessler, IJPP 2017; J. Supercomputing 2019. Four per-skeleton backends (C++/OpenMP/CUDA/OpenCL) with ML backend selection by operand size and an auto-tuned CPU/GPU split.
- Sequoia — Fatahalian et al., SC 2006. Machine modeled as a tree of memories; tasks are portable units decomposed to leaf granularity — the archetypal uniform machine abstraction with no privileged target.
- GA-based auto-offloading (NTT) — Yamato, Int. J. Parallel Emergent Distrib. Syst. 2021, arXiv:1806.01430 / 2002.12115. A genetic algorithm evolves per-loop offload decisions (GPU/FPGA/many-core CPU) evaluated by real measurement; emits OpenACC.
- DaCe / SDFG (the project's own substrate) — Ben-Nun et al., SC 2019, https://htor.inf.ethz.ch/publications/img/dace-sc19.pdf; GPU docs https://spcldace.readthedocs.io/en/latest/optimization/gpu.html. A Map scope (a parallel loop nest) carries a `schedule` (Sequential / CPU_Multicore / GPU_Device / FPGA_*); codegen emits per-target C++/CUDA/HLS from the same nest.

### Novelty verdict on the two framings

**(1) "A loop nest at a chosen granularity is the unit of offloading." Rating: none (as a standalone claim).** Automatic loop-nest-scale offload-unit selection is established by Polly-ACC (SCoP detection with a profitability + data-footprint heuristic and CPU keep-back, ICS 2016), PPCG (schedule-band kernels, TACO 2013), Baskaran C-to-CUDA (CC 2010), DawnCC (range-analysis-provable loops, TACO 2017), and NTT's GA search over per-loop offload decisions (2021). Inside the project's own framework the unit is already native: the DaCe Map scope is the retargetable loop-nest unit (SC 2019). The project must state which cut its "loop nest" is — perfect nest, PPCG-style outermost tilable band, or a DaCe schedule/storage boundary — because the primitive itself is inherited, not invented.

**(2) "CPU treated as just another accelerator target." Rating: none.** This is table stakes across a decade of portability work: OpenCL enumerates the CPU as a device (2008); Alpaka names the CPU backend `AccCpu*` (2016); Kokkos Serial/OpenMP and RAJA policies make CPU a peer execution space (2014/2019); OCCA selects a "Serial"/"OpenMP" device at runtime (2014); OpenMP treats the host as the default/fallback device (2013–2018); OpenACC compiles the same directive to `-ta=multicore` (2015); StarPU/Legion/PaRSEC make CPU cores peer workers/processors (2009–2013). In DaCe itself, `CPU_Multicore` and `GPU_Device` are already peer values of one `schedule` enum. Asserting this framing as a contribution would be refuted on sight.

### The one genuinely-unclaimed coordinate

The defensible novelty is a single conjunction, not either framing alone: **using the per-loop-nest offload unit as the carrier of a numerical-stability-gated sweep over {backend × compiler/source-language × numeric precision × vector length × emission strategy}, with the CPU enrolled as both the reference oracle and a co-equal candidate device in that same sweep, over a data-centric IR (DaCe) where CPU and GPU code are emitted from the same nest.** No surveyed system covers this:
- The autotuners that do search variants — StarPU perfmodel, Qilin, SkePU, PetaBricks, AutoTVM/MetaSchedule, OpenMPC's variant search — optimize runtime (and sometimes energy) and hold the numeric type fixed. None admits or rejects a variant on a numerical-accuracy gate. PetaBricks tunes accuracy-vs-time for variable-accuracy algorithms, which is the nearest neighbor and must be cited and distinguished: it trades algorithmic accuracy, not bit-level stability of a low-precision/fast-math lowering.
- None sweeps low precision (fp16/bf16/int) × vector width as offload/device decision axes. Portability layers fix dtype at the type level (SYCL/Kokkos/RAJA/Alpaka), and auto-offload compilers fix it as-written (Polly-ACC/PPCG/OpenACC/OpenMP). Precision-autotuning tools (Precimonious, ADAPT, FloatSmith) sweep precision but not device/offload and not per-nest offload granularity.
- None treats the compiler and source language as swept axes per nest. OCCA and DaCe emit to multiple backends but do not autotune the toolchain/language per loop nest.
- None runs the CPU as simultaneously the oracle baseline and a candidate device inside one precision/vlen sweep; device selection in GROPHECY/Qilin/NTT is a speed/energy model with no precision dimension.

The intersection (auto loop-nest unit ∧ uniform CPU/GPU emission ∧ per-unit precision × vlen × compiler × language cross-product ∧ hard numerical-stability gate) is the open niche.

### Risks and critique

"CPU as accelerator" is not novel and should never be presented as such. It is realized literally at the API level (OpenCL `CL_DEVICE_TYPE_CPU`, SYCL `device_type::cpu`), at the type level (Kokkos/RAJA execution spaces), in the naming itself (Alpaka `AccCpu*`), by a build flag (OpenACC `-ta=multicore`), and as a runtime device (OCCA, HTrOP). Most damaging for an intra-DaCe paper: the base framework already encodes it as a `schedule` enum value. Any reviewer will produce three or four of these citations immediately.

Granularity selection is largely PPCG/DawnCC/Polly-ACC re-warmed. Polly-ACC in particular already delivers the exact story of "auto-detect a loop-nest unit, decide GPU-vs-CPU per unit by a profitability heuristic, keep the loser on the CPU." Restating that as a contribution invites direct refutation. The project should concede the granularity primitive to DaCe/PPCG/Polly-ACC and argue only about what varies over the unit. Two specific weaknesses must be closed: (a) the paper does not currently pin its granularity cut against PPCG's outermost tilable band and DaCe's Map scope, so a reviewer cannot tell whether anything differs from Polly-ACC's SCoP; and (b) there is no precision-aware profitability/cost model for the host-vs-device choice — every prior cost model here (StarPU, Qilin, SkePU) is precision-blind, which is an opportunity but also an unfilled obligation if the project claims measured selection.

The measurement-driven arena idea is likewise anticipated in the abstract: NTT's GA offloading and StarPU/Legion's perfmodel/mapper already pick devices per unit by measurement or trained models. "We measure per loop nest" is not enough. The claim has to be that the measured objective includes a numerical gate and that the search space includes precision and vector width — dimensions absent from all of them.

### Effect on the recommended strongest paper (the numerically-guarded fast-math gate)

This axis strengthens that recommendation and does not dilute it, provided the framing is disciplined. Every system surveyed here fixes the numeric type and assumes cross-device behavioral equivalence, so a low-precision or fast-math GPU vectorization would silently change results with no admission control. That absence is uniform across uniform-device models, polyhedral auto-offload, and task runtimes — which is precisely the wedge for a numerically-guarded gate. The two framings this addendum demolishes ("CPU is an accelerator," "loop nest is the unit") should be demoted in the paper to inherited, well-supported design choices with citations (DaCe, Polly-ACC, StarPU/Kokkos/Legion), freeing the contribution to rest entirely on the gate. The gate is where none of the named prior art competes: it is the one criterion no surveyed selector uses, and it is what turns an otherwise-anticipated per-nest device/precision sweep into a defensible result. The risk to manage is scope inflation — pairing the gate with the cross-compiler/cross-language and precision/vlen axes is fine as the search space, but the paper's single load-bearing novelty sentence should be the stability gate, not the arena breadth, since breadth alone is covered piecewise by AutoTVM, SkePU, PetaBricks, and function multiversioning. Before finalizing, check ATF, BaCO, and ytopt for any existing accuracy/precision constraint in an HPC autotuner, since a hit there would be the only remaining threat to the gate's novelty.

## Addendum: Variant-Space Emission (Compilers Are Unreliable)

### The premise, checked

The claim that a single compiler and a single source form cannot be trusted to vectorize well is not a hypothesis; it is a measured fact with a decade-plus of supporting numbers. Maleki, Gao, Garzaran, Wong, and Padua (PACT 2011) ran GCC 4.7, ICC 12, and IBM XLC 11 over the 151-loop extended TSVC suite and found only 45–71% of the synthetic loops vectorized, and on real applications only 6–10 of 33 candidate loops vectorized; where the compiler failed, hand-vectorization recovered large residual speedups. Modern re-runs confirm the gap has not closed: the 2025 cross-compiler study (arXiv:2502.11906) reports x86 TSVC vectorization rates of GCC 54% / ICX 50% / Clang 46%, with only 35% of loops vectorized by all three compilers and 36% by none, and the fastest-compiler share flipping by platform (ICX 40% / GCC 39% / Clang 21% on x86 versus Clang 65% on ARM). The original TSVC (Callahan, Dongarra, Levine, SC 1988/1991) and TSVC_2 still show ~46–56% pass rates today.

The instability is driven by source form, not just compiler choice. Callanan et al. (ACM TACO 16(4), 2019) showed that withdrawing `restrict`, loop bounds, trip counts, or alignment information collapses vectorization across five production compilers (GNU, Intel, Clang, PGI, IBM) on four ISAs, meaning a single source form materially misrepresents what the loop can achieve. Gong et al. (OOPSLA/PACMPL 2018) mechanically generated semantically-equivalent source mutations (unroll, interchange, tiling, distribution) of >1000 loop nests and found best-variant search recovered ≥1.15x on 46.7% (GCC), 35.7% (ICC), and 46.5% (Clang) of loops, with per-loop runtime coefficient-of-variation across equivalent forms reaching ~119%. Finally, the compiler's own decision oracle is unreliable: Pohl, Cosenza, and Juurlink (MASCOTS 2019; PARCO 2020) measured LLVM's and GCC's internal vectorization cost models against real speedups over 85+ kernels and found predicted versus measured speedup only weakly-to-moderately correlated and systematically over-estimated.

State this honestly: the motivation is well-established and not novel. "Compilers are unreliable, and the same computation in a different source form vectorizes differently" is a published, repeatedly-confirmed observation, and it is already the standing justification for the entire flag- and source-autotuning literature.

### Prior art: the empirical autotuning tradition

"Generate many semantically-equivalent variants, measure them, keep the fastest correct one" is the defining move of autotuning and is 25+ years old:

- **PhiPAC** (Bilmes, Asanović, Chin, Demmel, ICS 1997) — first parameterized C matrix-multiply generator plus search scripts; tiling, unrolling, accumulator counts emitted as portable C, best selected by timing.
- **ATLAS / AEOS** (Whaley & Dongarra, SC 1998; Parallel Computing 2001) — formalized Automated Empirical Optimization of Software as exactly three variant-production mechanisms: parameterization, multiple-implementation, and code-generation, selected by empirical measurement.
- **FFTW** (Frigo & Johnson, ICASSP 1998; Proc. IEEE 2005) — machine-generated codelets composed into a plan chosen by dynamic-programming search over measured timings.
- **SPIRAL** (Püschel et al., Proc. IEEE 2005) — rewrites a transform into a space of mathematically-equivalent formulas, generates code, and searches with evolutionary/DP methods.
- **OSKI/Sparsity** (Im, Yelick, Vuduc, IJHPCA 2004) — autotuned sparse kernels including a data-layout (storage-format) variant axis, with runtime empirical search.
- **Halide autoscheduler** (Adams et al., SIGGRAPH/TOG 2019) and **TVM/AutoTVM/Ansor** (Chen et al., OSDI 2018; Zheng et al., OSDI 2020) — loop order, tiling, fusion, unroll, vectorize, and thread binding searched with learned cost models plus on-hardware measurement.
- **OpenTuner** (Ansel et al., PACT 2014), **CLTune** (Nugteren & Codreanu, MCSoC 2015), and **Kernel Tuner** (van Werkhoven, FGCS 2019) — domain-agnostic frameworks over user-defined spaces including tile/unroll/vlen and, in OpenTuner's case, compiler flags.

The project's variant space (loop order, tiling, unroll, data layout, reduction-tree shape, emission strategy, vlen) is the union of what these systems already enumerate.

### Novelty verdict

**None, as a standalone idea.** "Test many code variants because compilers are unreliable" is not a contribution. AEOS (2001) already formalized the exact three variant-production mechanisms this project uses; PhiPAC (1997), ATLAS (1998), FFTW (1998), and SPIRAL (2005) predate it by decades; OpenTuner (2014), CLTune (2015), and Kernel Tuner (2019) commoditized it into generic frameworks. On the source-form-because-compilers-are-unreliable axis specifically, Gong et al. (OOPSLA 2018) already mechanically generates equivalent loop-nest source variants and autotunes to the fastest form per compiler across GCC/ICC/Clang, and the multiple-compiler-ensemble line ("The Case for Multiple Compilers," HP Labs ~2004; "A Multiple Compiler Approach," UC eScholarship) already selects the best per-function binary across compilers with up to ~1.8x spread. Presenting measure-many-variants as a headline claim will draw a flat rejection.

### The defensible residual, if any

The residual is narrow and lies only in the intersection, which no single prior system occupies: **structural loop-nest variants × a cross-compiler-and-cross-language emission axis × per-variant numerical error measured against a bit-exact oracle, with a stability gate that decides which FP-semantics-changing variants are even admissible — all emitted from one data-centric IR (SDFG).**

Precisely why the autotuning canon does not cover this: FFTW, ATLAS, SPIRAL, Halide, TVM/Ansor, CLTune, and the polyhedral autoschedulers (Pluto PLDI 2008; Tiramisu CGO 2019) are all single-toolchain and treat floating-point associativity as either irrelevant or an illegal transformation. FFTW acknowledges that reassociation differs across plans but never ranks or gates on it; ATLAS, PhiPAC, CLTune, and Kernel Tuner check correctness only as a pass/fail tolerance filter (Kernel Tuner uses |out − ref| < atol + rtol·|ref|, FP16 1e-2 / FP32 1e-4), not as a ranking or admissibility signal; the polyhedral schedulers restrict themselves to exact-arithmetic-legal affine transforms and so cannot even produce a reassociated reduction. None measure each equivalent variant across multiple real compilers and languages while gating on error.

The one system that generates FP-semantics-changing variants and measures per-variant error against a high-precision oracle is **Herbie** (Panchekha, Sanchez-Stern, Wilcox, Tatlock, PLDI 2015), which rewrites scalar expressions and samples inputs against an MPFR oracle — but it optimizes for accuracy, not speed, operates on straight-line scalar expressions with no loops/tiling/vlen, and uses a single target. Its successors Pherbie (ARITH 2021) and OpTuner (CGO 2022) build accuracy-versus-speed Pareto sets but still at scalar-expression granularity with modeled, not measured-across-compilers, speed. STOKE-FP (Schkufza, Sharma, Aiken, PLDI 2014) couples performance with a stochastically-estimated max FP error, but on loop-free binaries for one ISA. PetaBricks variable-accuracy (Ansel et al., CGO 2011) makes accuracy a first-class autotuning objective but at algorithm level in a DSL. Precimonious (SC 2013) and GPUMixer (ISC 2019) search precision assignments under an error constraint, but precision is their only knob. Kernel Contracts (arXiv 2026) checks that autotuner schedules agree within rel-tol 1e-4 of a high-precision reference, but as a post-hoc contract check, not a generative arena. No system unifies error measurement with speed across multiple compilers and languages over structural loop-nest variants. That intersection is the only thing left to defend, and it must be defended empirically by showing the error gate *changes the winner* — that a fastest variant is numerically inadmissible at fp16 and a slower error-safe form is selected instead — rather than merely filtering, which is what Kernel Tuner already ships.

### Risks and critique

**Combinatorial blowup.** The space is the product of loop order × tiling × unroll × layout × reduction-tree shape × vlen × compiler × language × FP-mode × precision. The field deliberately abandoned measure-all for learned and analytical cost models (Halide 2019, Ansor 2020, AutoTVM 2018), and BLIS's analytical GEMM model (Low et al., TOMS 2016) argues a well-understood kernel needs no sweep at all. A pure measure-everything arena regresses on this unless the per-loop-nest scope keeps each space small, and that scoping argument must be made explicitly rather than assumed. An equality-saturation (egg, POPL 2021; TENSAT, MLSys 2021) or model-guided pruning story is needed or reviewers will cite scalability directly.

**Per-variant correctness cost.** Each variant now requires an oracle comparison, and that comparison is itself input- and platform-dependent (the OSKI and ATLAS lesson: the best variant depends on the input and the machine, and a numerical-error gate inherits that dependence). Sampled-input error is not a bound; sound static FP analysis (Daisy, FPTuner, Rosa) can catch worst-case error the arena will miss, so the metric must either adopt a sound bound or explicitly position itself as empirical-only and state the limitation. The oracle/metric choice (max relative error versus reliable-digit count versus Monte Carlo Arithmetic à la Verificarlo, ARITH 2016) must be justified, not assumed.

**This is Claim 1 restated.** The "compilers are unreliable, so emit many forms" argument is the motivation for the whole project, not a separable second contribution. Fold it into the framing rather than counting it as an independent novel claim; double-counting it invites the reviewer to reject it twice. The load-bearing novelty is the error-gated cross-compiler/cross-language selection, and the writeup should spend its evidence budget there.
