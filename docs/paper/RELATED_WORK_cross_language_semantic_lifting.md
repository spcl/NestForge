# Related Work: Cross-Language / Cross-Compiler Optimization and Semantic Lifting

## Cross-language compilation and optimization

A long line of work removes the compilation-unit and language boundary so that classical interprocedural optimization can act across it. LLVM cross-language LTO (LLVM Project, 2019, https://llvm.org/docs/LinkTimeOptimization.html) defers inlining, constant propagation, and dead-code elimination to link time, so a mixed Rust, C/C++, and Flang program is optimized as one module, provided every object is emitted as matched LLVM bitcode; the rustc linker-plugin-LTO path (rust-lang, 2018–2024, https://doc.rust-lang.org/rustc/linker-plugin-lto.html) exposes the same mechanism. MLIR (CGO 2021, https://mlir.llvm.org) generalizes the substrate rather than the link step: Flang, Polygeist, Mojo, and the ML frontends lower into shared dialects and optimize above LLVM IR, and the Transform dialect (2024, https://mlir.llvm.org/docs/Dialects/Transform/) scripts the schedule. GraalVM/Truffle (DLS 2015, https://www.graalvm.org), Swift/C++ interoperability (Swift 5.9, 2023, https://www.swift.org/documentation/cxx-interop/), and Julia's `ccall`/`llvmcall` dissolve the FFI call boundary so foreign calls inline without a transition.

What these approaches share is the object they optimize. Cross-language LTO works on the common, semantics-lossy LLVM IR into which each frontend has already baked its source contract, so C-origin code arrives without `noalias` facts, with FP association already fixed and provenance discarded, and it stays as constrained as its source; MLIR similarly carries each frontend's semantics down in its dialect. **What they do not do:** these systems cross the boundary for a single compiler, never re-derive a maximally-permissive contract before emission, and do not select among source languages or compilers by measurement.

## Verified lifting and raising

The intellectual lineage closest to our premise raises code out of restrictive source semantics into a more-optimizable representation. Casper (SIGMOD 2018, https://doi.org/10.1145/3183713.3196891) lifts sequential Java loops to a verified MapReduce summary and re-emits to Spark, Hadoop, or Flink; Dexter (SIGGRAPH Asia 2019, https://doi.org/10.1145/3355089.3356532) and STNG (PLDI 2016, https://doi.org/10.1145/2908080.2908117) lift C++ and Fortran kernels to Halide and stencil DSLs; Helium (PLDI 2015, https://doi.org/10.1145/2737924.2737974) recovers stencils from stripped x86 binaries. MetaLift (2023, https://metalift.pized.com) generalizes this machinery into a reusable synthesis-plus-verification framework, and Tenspiler (ECOOP 2024, https://doi.org/10.4230/LIPIcs.ECOOP.2024.20), LLMLift (NeurIPS 2024), and Guided Tensor Lifting (PACMPL 2025) extend it to tensor IRs with multiple hardware backends. In parallel, Polygeist (PACT 2021, https://doi.org/10.1109/PACT52795.2021.00016) raises C/C++ to affine MLIR and beats Polly and Pluto on Polybench (2.53x sequential, 9.47x parallel), Polygeist-GPU (PPoPP 2023, https://doi.org/10.1145/3572848.3577475) raises CUDA to portable parallel constructs, and mlirSynth (PACT 2023, https://doi.org/10.1109/PACT58117.2023.00012) raises low MLIR to higher dialects by synthesis. Progressive Raising (Chelini, CGO 2021, https://doi.org/10.1109/CGO51591.2021.9370332) formalizes the multi-step raise between dialects, and equality-saturation raising (Tensat, MLSys 2021; egg, POPL 2021, https://doi.org/10.1145/3434304) offers an alternative discovery mechanism.

Most directly, "Lifting C Semantics for Dataflow Optimization" (ICS 2022, https://doi.org/10.1145/3524059.3532386) lifts whole C programs into the same SDFG substrate we use, removing pointer aliasing by whole-program analysis, recovering parallelism no source compiler finds, emitting CPU/GPU/FPGA, and already relaxing floating point globally via `-ffast-math`. **This is our single closest prior art, and it is our own group's prior work.** We do not claim the lift. **What they lift and we do not** is the input side: they specialize in recovering a summary from arbitrary or even binary source. **What they do not do and we do** is on the emission side: each of these systems targets one downstream DSL, dialect, or device backend chosen by platform, and one toolchain (ICS 2022 uses gcc alone with a single global `-ffast-math`), whereas we treat the emitted language contract as a per-transform, per-array tunable and select the fastest correct variant across compilers. The delta over ICS 2022 is not the existence of a lift; it is the residual speedup that remains **after** an SDFG's existing `restrict` + `-fno-math-errno` emission, obtained by making association license and aliasing per-transform rather than global. That residual gap is the quantity our evaluation must report; without it the contribution reduces to "ICS 2022 with more flags."

## Knowing rather than recovering optimization facts: a soundness argument

We include this axis not to claim that "emitting a known fact" is itself novel — it is the generic, well-understood property of any lifted, dataflow, polyhedral, or DSL compiler, and DaCe, Halide, and ICS 2022 all already do it — but to establish **why our aggressive per-transform contracts are sound by construction**. The contrast with fact *recovery* is what licenses the knobs.

When the source drops a fact, a production compiler re-derives it at a lowered IR. LLVM LoopVersioning/LoopAccessInfo clones a loop under an O(pointers²) runtime disjointness check when alias analysis returns may-alias; Scalar Evolution (GCC and LLVM) reconstructs induction variables, trip counts, and dependence from scalar increments; TBAA (PLDI 1998, https://doi.org/10.1145/277650.277670) infers `noalias` from types; interprocedural points-to via LTO recovers cross-call aliasing; and `captures(...)` provenance attributes with GEP `nuw`/`nusw` flags (LLVM 2024) sharpen capture and non-negativity facts. Programmers can also inject facts through `llvm.assume`, `__builtin_assume_aligned`, and `#pragma ivdep`.

Three properties of recovery recur. Recovery is partial and fragile: SCEV needs canonical, affine, wrap-free loops, and TBAA is unsound in practice (widely disabled with `-fno-strict-aliasing`) and gives nothing for the same-typed `double*` arrays that dominate HPC. Precision does not scale: context-sensitive interprocedural analysis is deliberately dialed down in production. And a recovered fact must survive every later pass, which is why the full-restrict `@llvm.noalias`/`ptr_provenance` effort (LLVM Dev Mtg 2021) has not landed in six years and why alignment assumes have been measured to regress optimization (LLVM Performance Tips). The Limits of Alias Analysis (CC 2004, https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-540-24723-4_2) quantifies the diminishing returns of chasing precision.

A lifted, dependence-explicit IR makes all three moot, because disjointness, trip counts, strides, and access patterns are ground truth in memlets and symbolic map ranges. **This axis is not a contribution; it is the correctness backstop for the contracts in the next two sections.** What this literature does and we build on rather than duplicate is fact reconstruction from constrained lower IR; what the SDFG supplies is the guarantee that a contract we emit is entailed by explicit dataflow, so it needs no runtime check, no code duplication, and no defense against pass erasure.

## Language semantics as a performance barrier

A substantial evidence base explains why a gap persists even after an SDFG emits `restrict` and `-fno-math-errno`. Provenance-aware C memory models (Cerberus, POPL 2019, https://doi.org/10.1145/3290380; PNVI-ae-udi adopted as ISO/IEC TS 6010:2023) formalize the aliasing license compilers exploit, and N3058 recasts `restrict` in that model. Practitioner measurements make the cost concrete: a GEMV case study (0xfab.ch, 2024, https://0xfab.ch) reports 2.23 versus 11.21 GFlop/s between strict-aliasing C and `restrict`-qualified C. The fast-math literature (Walfridsson, 2021, https://simonbyrne.github.io/notes/fastmath/) enumerates errno, trapping math, signed zeros, and FP association as discrete reassociation and vectorization barriers, each licensed separately, and the correctness of relaxing them per-transform is discharged by verified compilation of floating-point optimizations (arXiv 2509.09019, 2025, https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.09019), which is the correctness anchor for our per-transform association license. Signed-overflow UB and strict aliasing are documented optimization enablers with measurable `-fwrapv` and `-fno-strict-aliasing` costs (GCC strict-overflow default change, 2017). Rust's `&mut`-to-LLVM-`noalias` mapping (rust-lang #82834, 2021, https://github.com/rust-lang/rust/pull/82834) demonstrates a language cashing its semantics out as an optimizer license, and its repeated miscompile-driven disabling shows that a blanket, language-wide license is correctness-fragile. Cross-language studies report that language choice alone moves performance for identical computations (Julia versus Fortran-MPI, GMD 2023; Rust sparse kernels, arXiv 2606.19213, 2026).

**What this literature does not do and we do** is turn the license into a per-transform, correctness-scoped emission choice driven by known dataflow rather than a global flag, a language-wide rule, or a hand-placed annotation. **The honest caveat that this same evidence forces on us:** the decisive datum in the 0xfab.ch study — GCC 14 refusing to vectorize the naive kernel while Clang 18 vectorizes identical source — is a **cross-compiler** effect, not a cross-language one, and its `restrict`-C figure of 11.21 GFlop/s shows that `restrict` in C recovers the Fortran-level performance we already emit. For LLVM-family targets, C-with-`restrict` and Fortran lower to the same `noalias` IR and the backend cannot distinguish them. The cross-language axis therefore does not stand as a co-equal headline; it is load-bearing only where a Fortran *default* unlocks a pass no C annotation can express (assumed-shape contiguity, whole-array/intrinsic recognition, allocatable aliasing beyond `restrict`) across differing compiler families, a narrow claim that requires a same-compiler-family ablation we treat as a secondary result rather than a motivating one.

## Empirical variant generation and selection

Generating many semantically-equivalent variants, measuring them, and keeping the fastest correct one is a mature methodology. FFTW (Proc. IEEE 2005, https://doi.org/10.1109/JPROC.2004.840301) and SPIRAL (Proc. IEEE 2005, https://doi.org/10.1109/JPROC.2004.840306) generate and time transform implementations; ATLAS (Parallel Computing 2001, https://doi.org/10.1016/S0167-8191(00)00087-9) empirically tunes BLAS. Superoptimization (STOKE, ASPLOS 2013, https://doi.org/10.1145/2451116.2451150; Souper, arXiv 2017, https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.04422) searches for the fastest instruction sequence proven equivalent within a peephole window. **What all of these do and we do not** is search over schedule, blocking, or instruction selection within one language and one compiler under fixed source semantics; **what we do and they do not** is make the emitted language contract itself the searched degree of freedom, so our selection loop is not new but the search space is.

## Cross-compiler portfolios and per-region selection

MCompiler (arXiv 2019, https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.03015; LCPC 2024) is the closest selection mechanism: it splits a C program into loop nests, compiles each with icc, pgcc, gcc, clang, Polly, and Pluto, times each variant in situ, and links the fastest per nest, using an ML model over hardware counters to predict the winner. Related points cover single axes: GCC/Clang function multiversioning (`target_clones`) selects ISA-specialized clones by runtime dispatch; OpenTuner (PACT 2014, https://doi.org/10.1145/2628071.2628092) and CompTuner (TOSEM 2024) autotune one compiler's flags; and a 2025 vectorization study (arXiv 2502.11906, https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11906) confirms no single compiler dominates, with ICX, GCC, and Clang each fastest on distinct loop fractions. Codee/Parallelware inserts `restrict`, `ivdep`, and `simd` directives per region across C/C++/Fortran, and FPTuner (POPL 2017, https://doi.org/10.1145/3009837.3009846) and Precimonious (SC 2013, https://doi.org/10.1145/2503210.2503296) tune mixed precision under an accuracy bound. The algorithm/schedule split of Halide (PLDI 2013, https://doi.org/10.1145/2491956.2462176) and Exo / Exo 2 (PLDI 2022, https://doi.org/10.1145/3519939.3523446; CC 2025) emits per-target imperative code from a permissive, alias-free specification, but the emission is one language per backend under one fixed semantics.

**What these portfolios do and we do not** is select over unmodified source (MCompiler feeds identical C to every backend, so aliasing, FP, overflow, and errno limits bind all variants equally) or manipulate the contract by source-level rule with no lifted facts and no cross-compiler race (Codee), or vary global switches and bit-widths rather than per-transform association license (the flag and precision tuners). **What we do and they do not** is vary the emitted language contract itself as the selectable degree of freedom, made sound by the lifted IR that supplies the facts, and gated on numerical correctness rather than on fastest observed time alone.

## Closest Prior Art and Residual Novelty

**1. "Lifting C Semantics for Dataflow Optimization" (ICS 2022).** The nearest system and our own prior work: same SDFG substrate, lifts arbitrary C, removes aliasing by whole-program analysis, recovers parallelism, emits CPU/GPU/FPGA, and already relaxes FP globally. Residual novelty of the *lift itself*: **none** — it is fully occupied, by us. Our delta over it is narrow and must be quantified, not asserted: the speedup remaining after its existing `restrict` + global `-ffast-math`, obtained by making aliasing and FP-association license per-transform and per-array, and by racing the result across compilers.

**2. Polygeist (PACT 2021).** Raises C/C++ to affine MLIR for polyhedral *scheduling*, single-language-in and single-toolchain-out. It does not emit a permissive language contract and does not select across compilers or languages. Novelty verdict: it occupies "raise to reschedule," which we do not claim; it leaves our contract-emission-and-selection conjunction untouched.

**3. MCompiler (arXiv 2019 / LCPC 2024).** Owns per-nest cross-compiler selection, but feeds identical unmodified C to every backend, spans no source language, manipulates no language contract, and checks nothing beyond fastest observed time. Novelty verdict: the selection *loop* is theirs and we credit it; our delta is *what varies inside the loop* — a lifted-IR-sound per-transform contract — and the numerical-correctness gate, neither of which MCompiler has.

The lift is conceded (system 1), the reschedule is conceded (system 2), and the selection loop is conceded (system 3). What no one holds is the conjunction of a per-transform, per-array, sound-by-construction language contract with cross-compiler empirical selection.

## The Opening

From a lifted IR whose dependence and aliasing facts are known by construction, we emit each loop nest under a per-transform, per-array maximally-permissive contract — `restrict`, alignment, induction-variable width and signedness, and a per-transform floating-point association license rather than a global `-ffast-math` — whose soundness is discharged by SDFG dataflow and whose result is gated on numerical correctness, and we empirically select the fastest correct variant across compilers; this per-transform correctness-scoped contract yields measurable speedup beyond an SDFG's existing global `restrict` + `-fno-math-errno` emission, and that residual gap is the falsifiable claim.
